Flow notes
  • Single 7-day grid is the only result view — no redundant stay list. ‹ › nav scrolls a week at a time, query refetches per page.
  • Each card carries DOW · date · site count, color-coded Available / Partial / Full.
  • Tapping a card selects it and pops the day detail below the grid: status line + min-nights stay info + action CTAs.
  • Available/Partial day → primary "Reserve on rec.gov →" deeplinks with date + nights pre-filled. Plus a secondary "Alert if more open" for partial days.
  • Full day → primary CTA becomes "🔔 Set alert" (one-tap to watch, one-tap to remove). The card itself shows a small bell badge so an active alert is visible at the grid level.
  • Min nights = 2 default. Arrival is the anchor; departure is implied by the selected card + min-nights.
  • "checked Nm ago · cached" + ↻ refresh sits above the grid for cache busts.

Availability history — two options

Both shown inside the day-detail context. Pick one as the lead, or stack them.

Fix the target slot. Plot how many sites have been open for this exact night over the last 60 days. "Has it been getting tighter?"

Data needed
  • Periodic snapshots of availability[date=Jul 14] for this campground. We're already polling for active alerts; could either snapshot every poll or do a daily background scrape per popular campground.
  • One row per (campground_id, target_date, observed_at) → small table, easy to bucket-aggregate.

My take
  • Lead with Option A (time series). It's the question users actually have when they're staring at a "Full" day: am I early enough, or did I miss it? Heatmap answers a planning question that comes earlier in the funnel — by the time they're in this drawer, they've already chosen the campground.
  • Heatmap is also colder-start: needs prior-season data we don't have. Time series can start working as soon as we have ~7 days of polling history per slot, which we'd accumulate naturally from active alerts.
  • Suggest building A first; revisit B as a season-summary section once we've collected a year of snapshots.
